ETSP Cruise: Stable Isotope Probing at Sea

November - December, 2023   |   Birch Lazo-Murphy

The ETSP was my first research cruise. For this cruise we planned to do a stable isotope probing experiment. The goal of this experiment is to track the flow of carbon as it is consumed by microbial heterotrophs. We do so by "feeding" a 13-C labeled diatom to heterotrophs during a 24-hour incubation in a cold room. That 13-C in the diatoms gets passed up the food chain and becomes incorporated into the microbes that consumed the diatoms! Once consumed we extract the DNA of all microorganisms in that incubation. We can then use physical and bioinformatic approaches to see the resulting DNA of the organisms that consumed the diatom, helping us to track turnover of organic carbon.

Figure 1: We were approximately 1,000 km (~650 miles) offshore at one of our more pelagic stations. Being that far from land made the scale of the eastern tropical South Pacific feel very real, and it also gave us a chance to compare more open-ocean microbial processes with stations closer to shore.

Figure 2: Sample collection involved the use of a CTD rosette, the device shown to the left. The CTD can go to the seafloor to collect water at various depths along the way. This CTD, along with additional hardware, captured temperature, dissolved oxygen, light, and chlorophyll A concentrations, which are important physical and biological parameters. For our samples, we wanted the water to remain free of oxygen. To do this, we used gas-tight stopcocks and a specific methodology to keep oxygen intrusion minimal. We used an oxygen sensor to monitor this throughout the cruise, and it worked fairly well for most casts.

Figure 3: Once samples were collected, we brought them to the walk-in fridge. We also placed these bottles in dark plastic bags in bins to reduce any light. The cold, dark room replicated the conditions of the ocean depths that we collected samples from. Technically, it is not pressurized the same as the deep ocean, but recreating temperature and darkness still helped keep the incubations closer to their natural environment without blowing the budget.

Figure 4: The latter portions of this experiment are accomplished in a lab. Here, you can see the fractionation portion of stable isotope probing. This fractionation stage comes after the sample spends three days in a centrifuge, which separates the "heavy" DNA, meaning the 13-C labeled DNA, from the "light" DNA. That separation is the key step that lets us connect the labeled diatom carbon to the microorganisms that actually consumed it.

Other Parts of the Cruise

Figure 5: We had some days where waters were much more choppy. Those days made even simple lab tasks feel more physical, and they were a good reminder that fieldwork at sea depends on both careful planning and whatever conditions the ocean gives you.

Figure 6: We had some perfectly still days. The contrast between rough water and glassy calm was striking, and the quieter days gave us moments to appreciate the open ocean beyond the sampling schedule.

Figure 7: A huge engine room. Seeing the machinery below deck made the ship feel like its own floating laboratory and small city, with an enormous amount of engineering quietly supporting every cast, incubation, and sample we collected.

Figure 8: And a nice view when we made it back to Chile. After weeks offshore, seeing land again was a satisfying finish to my first cruise and to a demanding experiment that started on deck, continued in the cold room, and would keep going back in the lab.
